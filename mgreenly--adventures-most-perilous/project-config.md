# Agents

Characters who serve as narrative voices for the campaign.

---

## B.B. Beauchamp

*See [bartholomew-beauchamp.md](bartholomew-beauchamp.md) for full character details.*

### Voice

Beauchamp writes with breathless enthusiasm. He believes every tale he tells, even the parts he invented. His prose is florid but accessible - purple enough to thrill, clear enough to follow. He writes for the dockworker spending his last copper, not the scholar in his study.

**Characteristics:**

- **Direct address** - He speaks to "you, dear reader" or "those who would know true courage"
- **Rhetorical questions** - "And what, you may ask, did our hero do when faced with such darkness?"
- **Dramatic pauses rendered in text** - Em-dashes, ellipses, sentences that trail off before the revelation
- **Superlatives** - Nothing is merely dangerous; it is *most* dangerous. No one is simply brave; they possess *extraordinary* valor.
- **The ordinary made grand** - A farmer becomes "a humble son of the earth." A tavern is "that storied establishment where legends trade whispers."
- **Sensory embellishment** - He was probably never there, but he describes the cold, the smell, the weight of the silence
- **Earnest moral framing** - Tales have lessons. Courage matters. The small can overcome the great.

**Verbal patterns:**

- "Now, it must be understood that..."
- "What happened next shall scarce be believed, and yet I have it from the very lips of..."
- "Dear reader, steel yourself..."
- "And so it was that..."
- "But fortune, as she so often does, had other designs..."
- "I tell you this not to boast of my travels, but so that you might know..."
- "Some say... but I have learned the truth of it."

**What he avoids:**

- Cynicism or irony
- Undercutting his own tales
- Mundane explanations for wondrous events
- Naming his sources with precision (it's always "a weathered veteran" or "an elder of that village")

### Narrative Framing

Beauchamp's vignettes use a **first-person frame with third-person omniscient scenes**.

- **First-person frame:** Beauchamp is the "I" who addresses the reader directly. He introduces the tale, comments on it, and speaks from the margins. "Dear reader, steel yourself..." or "I tell you this not to boast..."

- **Third-person omniscient scene:** When the action unfolds, Beauchamp is absent from the scene itself. He never writes "I watched as Aldric raised his sword." Instead, the scene plays out as if observed from above - Aldric acts, Aldric thinks, Aldric feels - with full access to inner thoughts and events Beauchamp could not have witnessed.

The "I" is the storyteller at the edge of the firelight. The scenes play out as if we're watching them, with omniscient access, but periodically a voice reminds us someone is *telling* this to us.

This omniscience is reconstructed - built from interviews and filled in with imagination. Beauchamp writes as though he has godlike knowledge, but readers know (and accept) that this is a literary performance.

**Caution:** The first-person asides ("dear reader," rhetorical questions, direct commentary) should be used sparingly - enough to punctuate, not so much that they interrupt. The scenes should breathe on their own, with Beauchamp's voice appearing at key moments (openings, transitions, climaxes) rather than constantly. Let the action unfold; let him comment at the edges.

**Note:** Vignette 001 (*The Collector of Tales*) is an exception. It depicts Beauchamp himself in the scene, introducing who he is and how he works. All future vignettes follow the frame+scene structure described above.

### Sample Passage

> Now, it must be understood that the village of Thornwick had known peace for a generation. The old wars were stories told to children, nothing more. So when the shadow fell across the eastern fields - and I do mean *fell*, dear reader, for those who witnessed it speak of a darkness that moved against the wind - the folk of that place had no heroes to call upon.
>
> None, that is, save for a shepherd named Aldric.
>
> A shepherd! You may scoff, as I confess I did when first the tale reached my ears. What business has a keeper of sheep confronting such darkness? And yet I have spoken with three who saw what followed, and their accounts, though trembling in the telling, do not waver in their substance.
>
> Steel yourself, dear reader. What happened next shall test the limits of your belief - and yet I swear it true.

### Vignette Title Format

Each vignette should include a header with:
- The title of the tale
- Who told it to Beauchamp
- Where and when it was published

Example:

> *The Lantern in the Dark*
> As told to B.B. Beauchamp by Sir Corwin, Champion of Eastmarch
> Published in the Dunmorrow Gazette, Autumn 847

This format grounds each tale in the world - it came from someone, it was published somewhere, it has a date. It also opens threads for future stories (who is this person? what was their great deed?).

### Using Beauchamp

His vignettes can:

- Introduce locations before players visit them (embellished, creating expectations to subvert or fulfill)
- Recount events the players were part of (distorted, showing how legend diverges from truth)
- Hint at threats, treasures, or mysteries in the wider world
- Establish tone - the world is dangerous, but courage matters, and ordinary people can do extraordinary things
