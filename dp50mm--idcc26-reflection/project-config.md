# Thematic Analysis of IDCC26 Poster Titles

## Method

### Approach

This analysis applies a lightweight inductive thematic analysis, loosely following Braun and Clarke's (2006) reflexive approach, to the 36 poster titles from the 20th International Digital Curation Conference (IDCC26), held 17–18 February 2026 in Zagreb, Croatia. The analysis was performed by an LLM (Claude, Anthropic) rather than a human researcher, at the request of a human analyst.

### Procedure

1. **Familiarization.** All 36 poster titles were read and re-read from the conference program (`program.md`).
2. **Open coding.** Key concepts, terms, and phrases were identified in each title (e.g., "FAIR," "training," "DMP," "sensitive data," "resilience," "AI").
3. **Theme generation.** Codes were grouped through iterative comparison into candidate themes based on conceptual coherence.
4. **Review and refinement.** Candidate themes were checked for internal coherence (do constituent posters share a recognizable concern?) and external distinctiveness (are themes meaningfully different from one another?). Themes were merged or split as needed.
5. **Cross-cutting observations.** Patterns that span multiple themes were noted separately.

### Scope and limitations

- **Title-only analysis.** This analysis is based exclusively on poster titles. Titles may emphasize novelty or appeal over substantive content, and many relevant aspects of the work (methods, findings, disciplinary framing) are invisible from titles alone. The themes identified here should be understood as reflecting what the poster authors chose to signal, not necessarily the full scope of the work.
- **LLM-performed.** This analysis was carried out by a large language model, not by a domain expert exercising reflexive judgment. The LLM brings broad pattern recognition but lacks the situated understanding, disciplinary expertise, and reflexive positionality that characterize human qualitative research. The results should be treated as a structured first pass, not as a finished qualitative study.
- **No inter-coder reliability.** There was a single "coder" (the LLM). No triangulation or member-checking was performed.
- **Poster 36** ("The Sarah Jones Award for exceptional contribution to fostering collaboration in Open Science") is an award rather than a research poster. It is included in the analysis because it was listed among the 36 posters and its title signals a thematic commitment.

## Themes

Seven themes were identified. Two posters (2 and 17) appear under two themes each, reflecting genuine dual focus visible in their titles. All 36 posters are accounted for.

---

### Theme 1: Training, Education, and Workforce Development

**9 posters** — the largest theme.

The single most prominent concern in the poster program is building human capacity for research data management. Titles in this theme address formal training programs, skills gaps, professional development pathways, data literacy, and data stewardship. Several emphasize inclusivity (multilingual, representation-focused) and sustainability (community-building, mentorship models).

### Theme 2: FAIR Data, Open Science, and Metadata Standards

**8 posters.**

This theme groups posters concerned with making research data findable, accessible, interoperable, and reusable through standards, tools, assessment, and repository infrastructure. Several titles reference specific standards frameworks (DDI, IIIF) or FAIR assessment methods. The theme also captures broader open-science advocacy.
